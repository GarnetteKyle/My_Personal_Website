404 Career Not Found: Spaghetti Life

Posted by: Garnette Kyle Valenzuela
January 29, 2026
Designed by Freepik

If my life as a Computer Science Student is a program, it would definitely be spaghetti code. Tangled, confusing, full of random if-else statements that don’t always make sense but somehow still runs, barely. Whenever someone asks, “So, what’s your plan after graduation?” I wish I could just hit Ctrl+Z and rewrite my entire answer. Because truthfully, I’m still debugging my career goals and the syntax is a mess.

Whenever people ask me what I want to be, I always answer confidently, “I want to be a software developer.” It sounds good, right? Professional, expert, tech wizard, technical, and just vague enough to sound like I know what I’m doing. But lately, I’ve started to realize that “software developer” isn’t just one thing. It’s a whole buffet of possibilities.

Designed by macrovector / Freepik

There’s the front end developer, the creative one who makes things look nice and interactive. Then there’s the back end developer, the mastermind who builds what happens behind the scenes, the magic that the user cannot see but actually makes everything work.

There’s the full stack developer, the mythical creature who somehow does both and survives. Then there are mobile developers, game developers, AI engineers, data scientists, and cybersecurity developers who spend their time protecting systems from the same hackers that movies make look cool.

So when I say “software developer,” what do I actually mean? Honestly, I don’t even know anymore. I feel like a kid in a Toys 'R' Us. Everything looks shiny and exciting, and I want to get it all. But I can't own everything. So here I am, like my code. Confused but running.

When I first chose BSCS, I thought it was simple. Learn to code, graduate, get a job, done. But the deeper I go, the more I realize this field is massive. Guess what, even AI is stealing my job. Each topic leads to another one, and suddenly you’re three YouTube tutorials deep learning something you didn’t even know existed.

And that’s the thing. The more I learn, the more I realize how little I actually know. It’s terrifying but kind of thrilling too. Every project, every assignment, every late night debug session makes me see a new side of computer science. Maybe that’s the beauty of it. There’s no single right path. Just like in programming, there’s more than one way to make something work. The "404" isn't a dead end, it is just a signal that I am exploring territory I haven't mapped out yet. I might not know where this path leads, but I am clicking "Run" anyway.

The only real mistake is the one from which we learn nothing.
